Guppy Multiple Moving Average (GMMA)

The Guppy Multiple Moving Average is one of the most powerful trend-analysis indicators used by professional traders to evaluate trend strength, market sentiment, investor behavior, and potential breakout opportunities.

Learn GMMA

What is GMMA?

Understanding the psychology behind market trends.

Guppy Multiple Moving Average (GMMA) was developed by Australian trader and author Daryl Guppy. Unlike traditional moving average systems that use only one or two averages, GMMA combines multiple moving averages into two groups.


The indicator visualizes the relationship between short-term traders and long-term investors. By studying the interaction between these two groups, traders can identify trend strength, trend changes, consolidations, and breakout opportunities.

Components of GMMA

Two groups of exponential moving averages.

Short-Term Trader Group

Represents active traders and market speculation.


EMAs:
  • 3 EMA
  • 5 EMA
  • 8 EMA
  • 10 EMA
  • 12 EMA
  • 15 EMA

Long-Term Investor Group

Represents investors and institutional conviction.


EMAs:
  • 30 EMA
  • 35 EMA
  • 40 EMA
  • 45 EMA
  • 50 EMA
  • 60 EMA

How to Interpret GMMA

Reading market sentiment using EMA relationships.

Strong Uptrend

Short-term EMA group remains above the long-term group with wide separation. Indicates strong buying pressure.

Strong Downtrend

Short-term EMAs stay below long-term EMAs, showing sustained bearish sentiment.

Consolidation

Both EMA groups compress and intertwine, suggesting uncertainty and reduced trend strength.

Trend Reversal

Short-term averages cross the long-term group, signaling a possible shift in market direction.

Major Trading Signals

How traders use GMMA for decision making.

1. EMA Expansion

Expanding distance between moving averages indicates increasing trend strength and trader conviction.

2. EMA Compression

Tight clustering of averages suggests weakening momentum and possible trend exhaustion.

3. Breakout Confirmation

A breakout accompanied by widening separation between the two groups often confirms a genuine trend move.

4. Trend Reversal Signal

When short-term EMAs decisively cross the long-term investor group, a trend reversal may be underway.

GMMA Trading Strategy

A practical approach used by many trend traders.

Step 1

Identify whether both EMA groups are aligned in the same direction.

Step 2

Wait for a pullback toward the short-term EMA cluster.

Step 3

Enter when price resumes movement in the trend direction.

Step 4

Exit if EMA compression develops or a crossover signals weakness.

Advantages & Limitations

Know where GMMA performs best.

Advantages Limitations
Excellent trend identification Can lag during rapid reversals
Visual representation of market psychology Produces noise in sideways markets
Useful for breakout confirmation Requires practice to interpret correctly

Best Practices

How professionals use GMMA effectively.

Combine With Volume

Volume confirms whether trend strength is supported by market participation.

Use Higher Timeframes

Daily and weekly charts often produce cleaner GMMA signals.

Follow Trend Direction

GMMA performs best when used as a trend-following tool.

Apply Risk Management

Always use stop losses and position sizing techniques.

Final Thoughts

The Guppy Multiple Moving Average (GMMA) is more than a moving average indicator. It provides a visual representation of the battle between short-term traders and long-term investors. By analyzing the separation, compression, and crossover of these EMA groups, traders gain deeper insight into trend strength and market psychology.


When combined with price action, volume analysis, support and resistance levels, and sound risk management, GMMA becomes a highly effective tool for identifying high-probability trend trading opportunities.
