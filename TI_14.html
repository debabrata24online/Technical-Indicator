RSI (Relative Strength Index)

A powerful momentum indicator used in technical analysis to measure price strength and identify overbought or oversold conditions

What is RSI?

The Relative Strength Index (RSI) is a momentum oscillator developed by J. Welles Wilder. It measures the speed and magnitude of recent price changes to evaluate whether an asset is overbought or oversold.

RSI values range from 0 to 100. It is widely used in stock, forex, and cryptocurrency trading.

How RSI is Calculated

RSI is calculated using average gains and average losses over a specific period (commonly 14 periods):

RSI = 100 - (100 / (1 + RS))

Where RS = Average Gain / Average Loss

Although the formula looks technical, most trading platforms calculate it automatically.

RSI Levels Explained

Overbought

70 - 100

Indicates the asset may be overvalued and could face a pullback or correction.

Neutral Zone

30 - 70

Market is in normal range with no strong overbought or oversold signals.

Oversold

0 - 30

Indicates the asset may be undervalued and could bounce upward.

How Traders Use RSI

  • Identify potential trend reversals
  • Confirm entry and exit points
  • Spot divergence between price and momentum
  • Combine with other indicators like Moving Averages or MACD

RSI Divergence (Advanced Concept)

Divergence occurs when price moves in one direction while RSI moves in the opposite direction.

  • Bullish Divergence: Price falls but RSI rises → potential upward reversal
  • Bearish Divergence: Price rises but RSI falls → potential downward reversal

Divergence is considered one of the strongest signals in technical analysis when confirmed with other tools.
